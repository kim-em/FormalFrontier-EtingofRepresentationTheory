Add to `EtingofRepresentationTheory/Chapter5/` (new file, e.g.

```lean
/-- A finite-dimensional polynomial GL_N-representation `M` whose
matrix coefficients are all homogeneous polynomials of degree `n` in
the entries `g_ij` (i.e. no `det⁻¹` factors and overall degree `n`)
admits a GL_N-equivariant injection into `(V^⊗n)^m` for some `m`,
where `V := Fin N → k`. -/
theorem polynomialRep_embeds_in_tensorPower
    {k : Type*} [Field k]
    {N n : ℕ}
    {M : Type*} [AddCommGroup M] [Module k M] [Module.Finite k M]
    (ρ : Matrix.GeneralLinearGroup (Fin N) k → M →ₗ[k] M)
    (hρ_alg : Etingof.IsAlgebraicRepresentation N ρ)
    (hρ_poly : <M is polynomial of degree n>) :
    ∃ (m : ℕ) (φ : M →ₗ[k] (Fin m → TensorPower k n (Fin N → k))),
      Function.Injective φ ∧
      <φ is GL_N-equivariant>
```

Exact statement is at the author's discretion. What matters:

1. The hypothesis pins down "polynomial of degree `n`" — either via
   homogeneity of the matrix coefficients in `Etingof.GLCoordVars` (no
   `D = det⁻¹` factors and overall degree exactly `n`) or via
   character homogeneity (`formalCharacter k N M` homogeneous of degree
   `n` in the eigenvalue variables).
2. The conclusion gives a concrete linear injection into a finite
   direct sum of tensor-power copies that is intertwining for the
   GL_N-action (where the tensor power carries the standard diagonal
   GL_N action `g ↦ g^⊗n`, which is `diagonalActionImage` in
   Theorem5_18_4.lean's setup).

### Why this is needed

Sub-issue #5 of the scoping doc (`polynomial GL_N-rep decomposes as
direct sum of Schur modules`) consumes this embedding to apply the
double-centralizer / Schur-Weyl decomposition of `V^⊗n` to a generic
polynomial rep. Without the embedding, we cannot reduce a polynomial
GL_N-rep `M` to its constituent Schur modules.

The book's argument (Etingof §5.23, around Theorem 5.23.2) is the
matrix-coefficient embedding: each matrix coefficient
`M_{a,c}(g_ij) ∈ k[g_ij]` is, by `IsAlgebraicRepresentation` and the
homogeneity hypothesis, a homogeneous polynomial of degree `n` in the
`g_ij`. Such polynomials are spanned by matrix coefficients of
`g ↦ g^⊗n` acting on `V^⊗n`. Iterating over the
`(dim M)`-many basis vectors of `M` gives the embedding into
`(V^⊗n)^{dim M}` (so a valid choice is `m := dim M`).

### Strategy options

**Approach A — matrix-coefficient construction.** Pick a basis
`(e_1, …, e_d)` of `M`. For each `e_a`, define
`φ_a : M →ₗ[k] (V^⊗n)` by sending `m` to the polynomial element
extracted from the matrix-coefficient `(g ↦ ⟨ρ(g) m, e_a^*⟩)` viewed as
an element of the degree-`n` symmetric power of the coordinate ring,
which is identified with `(V^⊗n)*` and hence (using the canonical
self-duality of finite-dim tensor powers) with `V^⊗n`. Bundle into
`φ : M →ₗ[k] (Fin d → V^⊗n)`. Injectivity follows because the
collection of dual functionals separates points.

**Approach B — Schur-functor / coalgebra-ring approach.** Use the fact
that `k[GL_N]` decomposes as a sum of `(V^⊗n)^* ⊗ (V^⊗n)` graded by
the degree, and apply duality. This is shorter conceptually but
requires more `MvPolynomial`/`PiTensorProduct` plumbing.

Approach A mirrors Etingof's exposition. Approach B is mathematically
cleaner but may require more Mathlib glue.
